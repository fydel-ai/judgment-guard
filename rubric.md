# Judgment Guard Rubric
## Evidence strength
High:
- multiple specific supporting facts
- source or concrete basis is visible
- no major unresolved contradiction
- evidence is current and not outdated by changed conditions

Medium:
- partial support
- some assumptions needed
- important unknowns remain

Low:
- mostly generalized reasoning
- little direct support
- strong claims built on weak premises
- evidence may be stale or from outdated context

## Assumption visibility
High:
- key assumptions stated explicitly
- load-bearing premises identified
- reader can see what the advice depends on

Medium:
- some assumptions implied but not called out
- premises partially visible

Low:
- assumptions invisible
- conclusions presented as though premises are self-evident

## Confidence calibration
Overstated:
- strong certainty words without support
- elegant causal chain with omitted caveats
- one-sided framing
- recommendation stronger than evidence

Appropriate:
- certainty matches evidence quality
- hedges present where needed, absent where not
- caveats included for weak spots without burying strong conclusions

Underconfident:
- excessive hedging on well-supported claims
- useful conclusions buried in qualifiers
- strong evidence presented tentatively

## Action pressure
High:
- "you should"
- ranked recommendation
- urgency framing
- executive-summary style conclusion

Medium:
- preference implied but not absolute

Low:
- exploratory and comparative

## Alternative coverage
High:
- multiple plausible options presented
- trade-offs compared
- no viable option ignored

Medium:
- some alternatives mentioned but not developed

Low:
- single option presented as obvious choice
- alternatives ignored or dismissed

## Epistemic authority
Appropriate:
- reasoning presented as general analysis
- no implied domain expertise
- defers to professionals where warranted

Overstated:
- implies insider or professional knowledge
- presents general reasoning as expert opinion
- fails to flag when professional advice is needed
